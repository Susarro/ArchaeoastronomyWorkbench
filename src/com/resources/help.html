Archaeoastronomy Workbench is a software that:

  • Provides geodesic, statistical and ghraphical tools for the study of the astronomical meaning of alignments recognised in archaeological sites either in relation to landscape or to the orientation of their structures and elements.
  • Allows to get the temporal evolution of equatorial (DE and AR) and horizontal (A and h) coordinates of Sun, Moon, the planets visible to the naked eye (Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter and Saturn) and some stars included in a catalogue in a graphical and numeric way.
  • Allows to calculate the date of heliacal and acronic risings and settings of the stars included a catalogue.
  • Allows to calculate two lunisolar archaeoastronomical targets: (a) the date of the first or last Moon since the occurrence of a solstice, equinox or cross-quarter festival [1]. (b) The date of coincidence in azimuths of the setting Sun and the full Moon, named Spring Full Moon or "Megalithic equinox" [2].
  • Allows to calculate the coincidence in altitude of a planet and a star in the rising or in the setting. Its aim is to resolve the coincidence in altitude of Saturn and Aldebaran accounted by Plutarch in De Facie in Orbe Lunae, 26: "Now when at intervals of thirty years the star of Cronus, which we call ‘Splendent’ but they, our author said, call ‘Nightwatchman,’ enters the sign of the Bull, they, having spent a long time in preparation for the sacrifice and the [expedition]" [3].
  • Allows to calculate the dates of occurrence of solstices, equinoxes and cross-quarter festivales for a given year.

Notes

In the future, it is important to note that, for us, equinox does not refer to the true equinox (i. e., when solar declination is zero) but the temporal midpoint between solstices[4]. In addition, year values are integers positives for years Anno Domine(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anno_Domini) (AD) and negatives for years Before Christ (BC). Year value 0 is equal to 1 BC. From 1582 AD calendar follows Gregorian rules while before that date, and even before 1 BC, calendar follows Julian reform[5]. Please, do not confuse Julian day with Julian calendar.
